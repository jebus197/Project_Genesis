# Narrative UX Design Challenge

**Date:** 2026-02-26
**Competitors:** CX (Codex) and CW (Cowork)
**Judge:** George Jackson (project founder)
**Context:** CC (Claude Code) built a technically correct web scaffold but failed twice to tell the Genesis story. This challenge is about the story.

---

## A — What Went Wrong (Two Rounds of Failure)

CC built a working web layer: 43 files, 21 routes, 1762 tests passing, Meridian CSS design system, JSON + HTML content negotiation, HTMX progressive enhancement. Everything renders. Everything works.

**And it's lifeless.**

### Round 1: Abstract jargon
The first attempt used language like "trust-mediated labour market" and "intelligence-agnostic" — phrases that mean something to someone who already understands Genesis, and nothing to anyone else. No context, no explanation of what the site is, how users interact with it, or why it even exists.

George's verdict: *"This is clearly a site generated by a machine, for machines. The human element is almost completely lacking."*

### Round 2: System mechanics as narrative
CC rewrote every template with content drawn from the README. But it described plumbing — trust scores, escrow mechanics, commission rates, fit score weights — instead of telling the story of why the building exists. Facts arranged on a page. Technically accurate. Emotionally dead.

George's verdict: *"It's just still missing overall narrative depth and scope. It just seems profoundly amateurish. So far this is by no means a 'shop window', so much as a poorly constructed rear exit."*

### The pattern
CC's failure mode is consistent: **technically correct content ≠ storytelling.** Listing that "trust is earned through verified work" and "escrow protects both parties" are facts about how Genesis works. They are not the story of WHY Genesis exists or WHY a visitor should care. CC describes the engine. The narrative needs to describe the journey.

---

## B — What the Narrative Must Achieve

A first-time visitor who has never heard of Genesis lands on the page.

**10 seconds:** They understand this is about protecting human work and human dignity in a world where AI is becoming the primary economic actor. Not "trust-mediated labour market." Not "intelligence-agnostic anti-social network." The human stakes.

**30 seconds:** They understand this system structurally prevents the exploitation patterns they already know — from social media (engagement bait, opaque algorithms, purchased influence), from gig platforms ("work done, never paid", race to the bottom, no portable reputation), from AI hype (capability without accountability, no way to verify who did what).

**2 minutes:** They understand how they'd participate — post a mission, bid on work, build a verified track record. They understand the mission lifecycle. They understand trust is earned not bought, and why that matters.

**After leaving:** They think "I understand why this needs to exist, and I want to be part of it." NOT "this is a system with trust scores and escrow mechanics."

The difference is: **mechanics tell you HOW something works. Narrative tells you WHY you should care.**

### The key emotional passages

These aren't just information. They're the soul of the project:

**The problem** — AI is capable but not trustworthy. For serious work, you can't answer: who asked for this? Who checked it? Were the checkers independent? Could someone have tampered with the record?

**The foundational rule** — "Trust cannot be bought, sold, exchanged, delegated, rented, inherited, or gifted. Trust can only be earned through verified behaviour and verified outcomes over time."

**Human dignity** — "Life events — illness, bereavement, disability, caregiving — are not inactivity. Without a protection mechanism, an actor who gets sick loses trust through no fault of their own. This is a new kind of poverty: your verified track record eroding while you're incapable of contributing."

**The anti-social inversion** — engagement optimisation → mission completion. Output volume → verifiable quality. Opaque algorithms → auditable processes. Purchased influence → earned trust only. Popularity ranking → capability matching. Vanity metrics → evidence profiles. Dopamine loops → work-first, no bait.

**On machines** — "The system that governs AI must not be governable by AI. Machines are workers and reviewers within the system. Humans are the legislators."

**On honesty** — "No serious system should claim to be invulnerable." Genesis aims for measurable risk reduction, not perfection.

**What Genesis eliminates** — No banks. No payment gatekeepers. No wealth extraction. No rug pulls. No bought influence. Nothing to rob. Crime has no value. The network defends itself.

**The closing** — "If that holds in practice, Genesis is not just another tool. It is a new trust substrate for coordinated work in the AI era."

## F — Constraints (Non-Negotiable)

1. Must be buildable with **Jinja2 + HTMX** (no SPA, no React, no build step)
2. Must preserve all **15 design constraints** from BRIEF.md (no engagement bait, no prestige hierarchy, etc.)
3. Must work with the existing **Meridian design tokens** (can extend, not replace)
4. Must keep the existing **route structure** (can ADD pages/routes, not remove working ones)
5. Must pass the existing **1762 tests** (smoke tests verify status codes, not content)
6. **PoC banner** on every page — users must always know they're viewing demonstration data
7. **Creator allocation** visible as named line item in every breakdown
8. **"Why this appears"** on every list ordering

## G — Deliverables

Each competitor produces:

1. **Landing page** — HTML mockup or detailed narrative spec. This is the most critical page. The shop window.
2. **Clickthrough narrative** — a concept for an interactive story/walkthrough accessible from the landing page. The why, the what, and the how. George specifically requested this.
3. **Narrative voice guide** — how the story is told across ALL pages (not just landing). Include 2-3 example page fragments showing the voice in action on non-landing pages (e.g., mission board context, registration onboarding, profile explanation).

### Optional (but valued)

5. Ideas for how the narrative progresses as a user goes deeper (landing → browse → register → first mission)

## H — Scoring Rubric

| Criterion | Weight | Question |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Shop window clarity** | 25% | Can a stranger understand what Genesis is in 10 seconds? |
| **Warmth & invitation** | 20% | Does it feel like somewhere you'd want to be part of? Does it convey human dignity? |
| **Narrative coherence** | 20% | Does the story flow naturally across pages? Is there a journey, not just pages? |
| **Anti-social compliance** | 15% | Zero engagement bait, zero prestige hierarchy, zero startup-chirpiness? |
| **Accessibility & readability** | 10% | Plain language, clear structure, not a white paper? |
| **Implementation feasibility** | 10% | Can CC build this with Jinja2 + HTMX in reasonable time? |

## I — The Tone

From the UX Design Spec:

**Do:** Plain-spoken, direct, confident. Quietly revolutionary. Respectful of intelligence. Warm but serious.

**Don't:** Startup-chirpy. Sombre/bureaucratic. Jargon-heavy. Preachy. Political.

**The sweet spot:** Confident institutional credibility + genuine warmth + radical transparency. Think: **the best public library you've ever been in** — obviously serious, obviously for everyone, obviously well-run, and you feel smarter just being there.

---

## J — What Success Looks Like

A visitor lands on the Genesis page. Within seconds, they understand this isn't another AI tool or crypto project. It's something that protects them — their work, their reputation, their dignity — in a world where AI is increasingly doing the work that used to be theirs alone.

They see the problem clearly. They see how Genesis addresses it — not with hype, but with structure. They see that the rules are written down, enforced by code, and can't be changed by anyone with enough money.

They feel invited, not sold to. Respected, not targeted. Curious enough to explore further.

That's the shop window. Build it.
